Methods Statement

I developed a multi-dimensional indexical scoring system analyzing 273 typographic signs across five districts in Price Hill, Cincinnati. Each sign received numerical scores on two dimensions:


First-Order Score (0–100) — Material Resources: Computed as the mean of three sub-scores: material durability (highest-durability material per sign, e.g., Paper=10, Metal=70, Neon=90), making investment (highest-skill fabrication mode, e.g., Handwritten=15, Printed=60, Chiseled=83), and scale/visibility (largest size dimension, e.g., Tiny=0, Large=67, Huge=100).


Second-Order Score (0–100) — Social Accessibility: Computed as the mean of four sub-scores: linguistic accessibility (monolingual=33, bilingual=67, trilingual=100), genre accessibility (coded 15–95 by legibility/universality of use context), visual accessibility (Image only=100, Text+image=67, Plain text=50, Complex typestyle=33), and discourse openness (Private=15, Commercial=85, Regulatory/Infrastructural=90–92).


Signs were classified into typological patterns using thresholds of 45 (First-Order) and 55 (Second-Order): Resilient Vernacular (low resources, high accessibility), Inclusive Investment (high resources, high accessibility), Compound Fragility (low resources, low accessibility), and Exclusive Investment (high resources, low accessibility).
